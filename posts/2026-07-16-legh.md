## Risks and bear case

**Assume the thesis failed. Most likely reasons:**

1. **The workforce housing orders are a one-off, not a new channel.** The ~600 units of data-center-linked orders represent a single customer or project; once delivered, LEGH returns to the tepid "good old-fashioned mobile home business" that Hodgson himself describes as "rather tepid" in Georgia. There is no durable new demand channel -- just a lumpy order that flatters Q2-Q4 2026 and then falls away.

2. **The loan book has hidden credit risk.** The 97% current rate and 4% repo rate look clean, but the loan book is largely chattel lending to subprime/near-prime borrowers at 13.1% rates -- precisely the population that deteriorates first in a downturn. The $48.6M bridge loan (maturing July 2026, Louisiana MH parks) is a concentrated risk. The allowance for loan losses may be understated, especially given the material weakness in internal controls. If credit normalizes to 6-8% losses, interest income gets eaten by provisions.

3. **The material weakness is a real governance problem.** The auditor's adverse opinion on internal controls is not cosmetic -- it means the financial statements may contain undetected errors. This could suppress the multiple indefinitely and raises the question: if internal controls are weak, how reliable are the loan book's reported delinquency rates?

4. **Section 45L expiration raises the tax rate.** The energy credit terminates June 30, 2026. Q1 2026 had a 16.1% effective rate; H2 2026 normalizes toward 21%+. The ~500bps tax headwind reduces after-tax earnings by ~$2-3M annually -- a material hit to a $42M NI company.

5. **Tariffs and input cost inflation compress margins.** New Section 232 duties on aluminum, steel, and copper + persistent tariff costs (~$1,200/unit) pressure already-thin manufacturing margins. Without pricing power (affordable housing buyers are price-sensitive), this squeezes the product business.

**Smartest bear:** "LEGH is a small, thinly-covered manufactured housing builder with an auditor-flagged material weakness, a failed acquisition (AmeriCasa), declining unit volumes, a self-originated subprime loan book with opaque credit quality, and a tax credit that just expired. The stock is at book value because that's what it's worth -- a mediocre business with governance risk. The workforce housing orders are a sugar high."

**My rebuttal:** The bear is right that the manufacturing business is cyclically weak and that the material weakness is a legitimate concern. But the bear underweights: (a) the loan book is $430M yielding 8-13% with ZERO external leverage, which means credit losses would have to be catastrophic (>10%+ annually) to impair equity -- and the 97% current rate on 3,800+ loans with collateral (the homes) does not suggest catastrophic loss levels; (b) the workforce housing orders have $8M in non-refundable deposits -- these are not hypothetical; (c) book value has compounded from $12.74 to $22.66 in 5 years through a full cycle, demonstrating the model's durability; and (d) 54% insider ownership aligns management with shareholders on the downside. The material weakness needs monitoring but has not affected the auditor's opinion on the financials themselves (unqualified opinion on the statements; adverse only on controls).
