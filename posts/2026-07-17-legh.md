## Current view

**Verdict:** Hold the existing position, but do not add. **Conviction:** 4/5 for the existing holding and 3/5 for a new purchase. I would wait for the July bridge-loan outcome and the second-quarter filing before initiating or adding.

## Variant view

Legacy Housing is usually valued as a small, cyclical manufactured-home producer. The better description is a modest manufacturing business attached to a large, on-balance-sheet loan portfolio. The market appears to expect earnings power to remain near the fiscal 2025 trough because workforce housing is a one-off and the lending assets deserve little more than inventory value. I think that is too pessimistic: the loan book can keep earning through a weak home-production cycle, while the deposited workforce order offers a path to higher factory utilization without requiring a broad housing recovery.

The discount is understandable. The workforce order comes from one customer, Legacy had shipped no units under it by March 31, the largest mobile-home-park loan matures in July, repossessed inventory is rising, and internal controls remain ineffective. The opportunity exists only if the lending book remains sound and the workforce order becomes product revenue rather than a temporary cash receipt.

## Business and financial position

Legacy makes and sells manufactured homes and finances consumers, dealers and mobile-home-park operators. At March 31, 2026, the four filed gross-principal balances totaled an analyst-calculated $437.429 million: $204.822 million of consumer loans, $199.542 million of mobile-home-park notes, $26.461 million of dealer notes and $6.604 million of other notes. The consumer and park books carried filed average contractual rates of 13.1% and 8.2%, respectively. The 2025 Form 10-K reports $9.138 million of consumer loans acquired with AmeriCasa. That proves the portfolio is not wholly self-originated; the filings reviewed do not establish the origin of every remaining loan. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, Notes 3–6](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm); [FY2025 Form 10-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926027133/legh-20251231x10k.htm)

First-quarter product sales were a filed $21.550 million, and loan-portfolio interest included in revenue was a filed $11.318 million. Product sales less filed product costs yields a 30.69% product gross margin, an analyst calculation rather than a company-reported consolidated margin. The quarter produced filed operating income of $12.389 million, net income of $10.928 million and diluted EPS of $0.46. Fiscal 2025 diluted EPS was a filed $1.74. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, statements of income](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm); [FY2025 Form 10-K, Note 17](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926027133/legh-20251231x10k.htm)

The balance sheet is unusually lightly levered. Filed March 31 amounts were $14.111 million of cash, $0.899 million drawn on a $50 million credit line, $52.479 million of total liabilities and $538.993 million of stockholders’ equity. Using the filed 23,781,601-share count produces analyst-calculated book value of $22.6643 per share. Subtracting filed goodwill of $2.472 million and net intangible assets of $4.956 million produces analyst-calculated tangible book value of $22.3519 per share. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, balance sheet and Note 11](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

Reported book value is not a liquidation value. The filing gives paired carrying and estimated fair values for three explicitly fixed-rate/disclosed classes: consumer loans at $199.325 million versus $180.144 million, fixed-rate park notes at $179.655 million versus $173.737 million, and other notes at $6.336 million versus $6.199 million. Those classes total an analyst-calculated $385.316 million of carrying value and $360.080 million of fair value; their filed class shortfalls of $19.181 million, $5.918 million and $0.137 million sum to a $25.236 million fixed-rate-subset gap. Variable-rate park notes have $17.340 million of carrying value but no exact filed fair value; Note 14 says they approximate carrying value. The exact total-portfolio fair value is therefore unresolved, and no zero gap is inferred for that class. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, Note 14](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

## Why the market may be wrong

The finance operation is already economically important: its filed first-quarter portfolio interest exceeded half of product sales. Filing-derived book value per share rose from analyst-calculated $12.7798 in 2021 to $22.1992 in 2025, a 14.80% CAGR; it reached $22.6643 in the first quarter of 2026. Measured separately, the 2022-to-first-quarter-2026 annualized rate is 12.00%. These are analyst calculations from filed equity and share counts, not targets. [FY2022 Form 10-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000155837023003920/legh-20221231x10k.htm); [FY2025 Form 10-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926027133/legh-20251231x10k.htm); [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

The possible manufacturing inflection is tangible but unproved. The company had received a filed $7.1 million non-refundable deposit from one workforce-housing customer and had begun production, but it had recognized no related shipments or revenue by March 31. The filing does not disclose order revenue, unit count or margin. Repeat demand therefore remains an open thesis test rather than a filed fact. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

## The full bear case

The biggest risk is that the delinquency tables look cleaner because troubled loans migrate into repossessed homes. Consumer loans 90-plus days past due were a filed 1.4% and park loans were 1.0%, yet consumer repossessed-home inventory rose from a filed $8.4 million at year-end to $10.6 million in the first quarter. Combining consumer 90-plus-day principal and repossessed inventory produces a 6.27% stress ratio, an analyst calculation. The analyst sum of the four filed loan-roll-forward provision lines is $1.139 million; the cash-flow statement separately reports a filed $1.121 million provision for accounts and notes receivable. The $0.018 million difference is unreconciled and analyst-calculated. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, Notes 3–6 and 9](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

Concentration makes that migration risk more dangerous. One $48.6 million, 7.9% interest-only park loan—roughly one-quarter of filed park principal—matures in July 2026. If it is extended on weak terms, stops accruing, or takes a principal loss while repossessions rise, the lending platform can destroy the book-value support that makes the stock look safe. The workforce deposit could still create a short revenue spike without becoming a repeat channel. Ineffective internal controls and provisional AmeriCasa purchase accounting increase the chance that deterioration is recognized late. In that outcome, reported book value is not a floor. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, Note 4](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

The company’s low borrowing helps it survive a normal credit cycle, but survival is not the same as preserving equity value. A bridge impairment, persistent repossession growth and a one-customer manufacturing order that does not repeat would turn the apparent finance-platform advantage into a credit-and-governance value trap.

## What would change the view

The following are analyst-selected failure thresholds, not company guidance. The view would be wrong or materially impaired if consumer 90-plus-day past due reaches 5.0%, park 90-plus-day past due reaches 3.0%, or the four-book quarterly provision reaches $2.5 million. It would also be wrong if the analyst-calculated consumer stress ratio reaches 7.50%; if consumer repossessed inventory reaches more than $12.72 million—greater than 20% above the $10.6 million baseline—while controls remain ineffective; or if the $48.6 million note suffers principal loss, nonaccrual or impairment. [Q1 2026 Form 10-Q, Notes 3–6 and 9](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1436208/000110465926057062/legh-20260331x10q.htm)

The manufacturing case fails if second-quarter product sales are below $30 million or total units are below 350. The repeat-channel claim fails if cumulative workforce shipments are below 300 by the third-quarter report, the order is cancelled or materially reduced, or the deposit is recognized outside product revenue. Two consecutive quarterly declines in book value per share, an operating margin below 25%, or a wider $25.236 million three-class fixed-rate-subset fair-value gap without a corresponding rate move would also contradict the expected compounding.

The analyst-estimated probability-weighted price is $32.50: 25% × $40 plus 50% × $34 plus 25% × $22. That is a 19.18% expected return. Annualized over the 14-month base horizon, it is 16.23%, versus a 9% benchmark assumption. No dividend is assumed. The $22 bear estimate is a scenario outcome, not a floor; a bridge loss or controls-driven credit surprise could produce a lower result.

The next decisive evidence is the July bridge-loan resolution and the second-quarter filing. A constructive result needs all three elements: credible bridge repayment or sound renewal terms, filed workforce conversion and credit measures that stay below the failure levels above. Until then, the upside case is plausible, but not proven.
